1 · What is a hydrograph?

A hydrograph is a time-series of discharge — usually m³/s on the y-axis and hours or days on the x-axis. Scroll the panel on the right to walk through the three named regions.

Step 1 · Rising limb

After rain falls, the channel doesn't respond instantly — water has to reach the stream. The discharge climbs steeply: this is the rising limb. Steeper limb → flashier catchment (small, urban, saturated).

Step 2 · Peak

The flow tops out at the peak discharge — the largest instantaneous flow during the event. Lag time is the delay between the rainfall centroid and this peak. For flood design, the peak is everything.

Step 3 · Falling limb (recession)

Once rain stops, the channel drains. The falling limb, or recession, often follows a near-exponential decay set by catchment storage. Slow recession → big groundwater reservoir; fast recession → little storage.

Why this matters The shape encodes catchment memory. Flashy = small / impervious / wet. Damped = large / forested / dry. It's the entry skill for flood forecasting, recession analysis, and model calibration.

3 · Baseflow separation — the Lyne–Hollick filter

Real baseflow isn't a straight line. The Lyne–Hollick filter [1] runs three forward/backward passes of a one-parameter recursion:

ft = α · ft−1 + (1+α)/2 · (Qt − Qt−1)
bt = Qt − max(ft, 0)

Drag α inline: → higher values strip more quickflow, giving a smoother, lower baseflow line. BFI = baseflow volume ÷ total volume.
