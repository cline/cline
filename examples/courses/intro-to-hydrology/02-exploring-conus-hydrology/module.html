4 · Reading the annual regime

  

    The lower panel of the plot shows the annual regime — mean discharge for each calendar month,
    averaged over the whole record. Each gauge has a distinctive signature shape:
  


  

    

      🌊 Fish River (ME) — broad spring peak (snowmelt + rain), summer low,
      occasional winter spikes from rain-on-snow. Classic mixed rainfall-snowmelt regime.
    

    

      ❄️ Siletz River (OR) — deep winter peaks (Pacific frontal rain + snowmelt),
      extremely dry August–October. Classic Pacific maritime regime.
    

    

      🏜️ Rillito Creek (AZ) — near-zero most of the year with sharp July–September
      monsoon spikes. Zero-to-flood in hours. Classic semi-arid flashy regime.
    

    

      🌿 Bogue Chitto (LA) — gentle, year-round flow with a slight winter–spring
      maximum. Nearly flat regime. Classic groundwater-dominated regime.
    

  


  

    What to look for
    After running the cell, compare the regime bar chart to these descriptions.
    A narrow, spiky regime (Rillito) vs. a smooth, broad one (Bogue Chitto) tells you
    how quickly the catchment responds and how much groundwater storage it has.
  









  
5 · Spotting a flood event

  

    In the cell output, flood days are shaded amber — days where discharge exceeds the
    95th percentile threshold of the whole record. This is a simple but robust
    peak-over-threshold (POT) approach used in operational flood studies.
  


  

    

      
Why P95?

      

        The 95th percentile means only 5% of all days exceed it. On a 5-year record that's
        ~91 days — spread across several distinct flood events. Lowering the threshold captures
        minor high-flow days; raising it isolates only extreme events.
      

    

    

      
Return period

      

        The peak printed below the plot is the multi-year maximum. Dividing the record length
        by the number of exceedances gives a rough return period estimate — useful context
        for flood risk but needs longer records for reliability.
      

    

  


  

    Try this
    Switch the gauge to Rillito Creek (AZ) and re-run. The amber flood bars should
    concentrate tightly in July–September (monsoon season). Compare that to Fish River (ME)
    where flood bars scatter across winter–spring. This is regime diversity in action.
