1 · What makes a hillslope cell wet?

Water flows from high cells to low cells under gravity. A cell tends to stay wet when:

  • Many upslope cells drain into it — the contributing area a is large.
  • The local slope is gentle — water lingers rather than rushing away, so tan(β) is small.
Intuition Big drainage area + gentle slope = wet.
Tiny drainage area + steep slope = dry.
TWI turns that sentence into a number [1].
Real-world implication High-TWI zones correspond to saturated-excess runoff areas, riparian wetlands, and ephemeral stream initiation points [3].

Under the hood: three things worth understanding

Why ln() — not just a / tan(β)?

The raw ratio a / tan(β) spans roughly 4 orders of magnitude across a real catchment — a valley floor might have a/tan(β) ≈ 40,000 while a steep ridge has ≈ 5. Plotting those values linearly produces a map where virtually every cell looks the same shade and only the valley floor is visible.

Taking the natural log compresses the range to roughly 2–14 (a 12-unit spread), where gradients are perceptible everywhere on the hillslope. It also has a physical basis: Beven & Kirkby (1979) derived TWI directly from the steady-state soil-moisture deficit equation — the ln falls out of the algebra, it is not a cosmetic choice.

A practical bonus: at very low slopes (tan(β) → 0, common in flat valley floors), a / tan(β) diverges to infinity; ln keeps the index finite and interpretable with a small epsilon floor.

D8 vs D∞ routing algorithms

D8 (O'Callaghan & Mark, 1984) routes 100% of each cell's flow to the single steepest of its 8 cardinal/diagonal neighbours. It is fast and deterministic, but forces flow into only 8 discrete directions — producing "stair-step" channel artefacts and overestimating channelisation on smooth hillslopes.

D∞ (Tarboton, 1997) computes the steepest downslope direction as a continuous angle, then splits flow proportionally between the two neighbours bounding that angle. This allows any of 2π possible directions, producing smoother contributing-area fields and more realistic distributed wetness patterns — but at higher computational cost.

For channel network extraction D8 is usually preferred (discrete routing matches the physics); for spatially continuous TWI fields, D∞ reduces artefacts. The AI-Hydro compute_twi tool defaults to method="d8" but accepts method="dinf".

Resolution dependence — why you must report DEM resolution

Specific contributing area is a = A_upslope / w where w is the contour width (≈ cell size in raster DEMs). At coarser resolution each cell accumulates the same physical drainage area but w is larger — so a is proportionally larger, and TWI inflates.

Sørensen et al. (2006) [3] quantified this: mean TWI varied by 1–2 units between 25 m and 200 m grids of the same catchments — a shift large enough to change wetness-class assignments. This means TWI values from different studies are not directly comparable unless computed at the same resolution.

Practical guidance: 30 m (SRTM, 3DEP-30) is the standard for continental-scale CAMELS comparisons; 10 m (3DEP-10) for sub-catchment studies. Always cite the source DEM and its resolution when reporting TWI statistics.

6 · Synthetic catchment TWI heatmap

  
A 16×16 synthetic hillslope — contributing area peaks in the central valley, slope tapers downhill. Run to see where wetness concentrates.


  

    

      python
      matplotlib TWI heatmap + distribution
      
    

    
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import matplotlib as mpl
mpl.rcParams.update({
    'figure.facecolor':'#0a0a15', 'axes.facecolor':'#0f0f1e',
    'text.color':'#7dd3fc',       'axes.labelcolor':'#7dd3fc',
    'xtick.color':'#94a3b8',      'ytick.color':'#94a3b8',
    'axes.edgecolor':'#334155',
})

n = 16
y_idx, x_idx = np.mgrid[0:n, 0:n]
area_grid  = 50 + 200 * np.exp(-((x_idx - n/2)**2) / 18) * (1 + y_idx / n)
slope_grid = 18 - 12 * (y_idx / n) + 4 * np.abs(x_idx - n/2) / n
twi_grid   = np.log(area_grid / np.maximum(np.tan(np.deg2rad(slope_grid)), 1e-6))

fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(8, 3.6))
im = ax1.imshow(twi_grid, cmap='GnBu', origin='lower')
ax1.set_title('TWI grid', fontsize=11)
ax1.set_xlabel('x'); ax1.set_ylabel('y (↑ downslope)')
cb = fig.colorbar(im, ax=ax1, fraction=0.046); cb.set_label('TWI')

ax2.hist(twi_grid.ravel(), bins=20, color='#00A3FF', edgecolor='#0a0a15', alpha=0.85)
ax2.axvline(twi_grid.mean(), color='#00FFFF', linewidth=1.5, linestyle='--')
ax2.text(twi_grid.mean()+0.1, ax2.get_ylim()[1]*0.9,
         f'mean={twi_grid.mean():.1f}', color='#00FFFF', fontsize=9)
ax2.set_title('TWI distribution', fontsize=11)
ax2.set_xlabel('TWI'); ax2.set_ylabel('count')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
print(f"Grid mean TWI = {twi_grid.mean():.2f},  std = {twi_grid.std():.2f}")
print(f"Dry (<5): {(twi_grid<5).sum()}  |  Wet (>8): {(twi_grid>8).sum()}")
