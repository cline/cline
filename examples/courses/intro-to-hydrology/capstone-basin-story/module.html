💧 Module 1 · The Water Cycle

      
A water molecule evaporates from the ocean, condenses in clouds, falls as precipitation over your basin, and begins its journey.

      
For Fish River, ME, much of the annual precipitation (~1 000 mm/yr) arrives as rain and snow. The six-flux budget: P ≈ ET + R + ΔS constrains everything that follows.

      
Key concept re-usedP = ET + R + ΔS. The mean Q you just computed is R expressed in m³/s.

    


    
    

      
🏔️ Module 2 · The Watershed

      
Every drop that falls inside the Fish River drainage divide eventually passes through gauge 01013500. Topography routes it there via D8 flow paths.

      
The watershed boundary is an invisible wall drawn by elevation — the continental divide concept applied at sub-basin scale. Flow paths converge in valleys; ridges are the divides.

      
Key concept re-usedContributing area (a) from Module 2 feeds directly into the TWI formula: TWI = ln(a/tan β).

    


    
    

      
📈 Module 3 · Reading the Hydrograph

      
The multi-year record you just plotted is a sequence of storm hydrographs. In Fish River, ME you'll see steep rising limbs in spring (snowmelt) and scattered winter peaks (rain-on-snow events).

      
The flashiness of the rising limb depends on catchment storage. A high BFI basin (like Bogue Chitto) barely shows individual storm peaks — groundwater smooths them out.

      
Key concept re-usedLag time and recession shape are encoded in the flashiness index and the FDC slope you computed in §3.

    


    
    

      
🌊 Module 4 · Baseflow Separation

      
The Lyne–Hollick filter extracted the slow groundwater component from your record.
      Your basin's BFI is ≈ 0.55 — meaning roughly 55% of all discharge comes from stored groundwater, not direct storm runoff.

      
A BFI above 0.7 indicates a basin where soil or aquifer storage dominates (e.g. Bogue Chitto, LA). A BFI below 0.2 signals rapid direct runoff with minimal storage (e.g. Rillito Creek, AZ).

      
Key concept re-usedα = 0.925 (3 passes) is the standard Lyne–Hollick default — same filter you used in Module 4.

    


    
    

      
📡 Module 5 · Real Streamflow Data

      
In Module 5 you found gauge 01013500 on the USGS NWIS map.
      Here you've extended that record to 10 years to see inter-annual variability — wet and dry years,
      flood years and drought years.

      
The annual regime (bar chart in §2) is the most compact summary of how your basin behaves across seasons — a fingerprint unique to its climate and landscape.

      
Key concept re-usedP95 flood threshold from Module 5 reappears in §2 to count flood days in the longer record.

    


    
    

      
🌱 Module 6 · DEM → TWI

      
The TWI grid from §4 tells you where saturation-excess runoff is most likely.
      Your basin's mean TWI of ≈ 8.2 places it in the
      moderately wet class.

      
This completes the spatial picture: the hydrograph tells you when flow is high; the TWI tells you where the landscape was already saturated when it happened.

      
Full synthesisTogether — BFI + FDC + regime + TWI — these four numbers tell your basin's water story from precipitation to sea.
